### Workforce Support, Retention, and Program Impact – Executive Summary

## Objective
This work is a cohesive analytics story about how workplace systems shape performance, retention, and well‑being. The goal is to show how I go from “interesting people data” to concrete, financially grounded recommendations that an operator or exec could actually act on.

## Key Findings
1) Structured Support Isn’t Just “Nice to Have” – It Moves the Needle
Employees in structured support models consistently outperform those with light or no support on weekly productivity.

The lift holds even after accounting for differences in tenure and workload, which suggests the program is doing real work, not just selecting already strong performers.

When I translate that lift into dollars and net it against program cost, the structured support model delivers a strong positive ROI, which frames this as a revenue‑relevant decision, not just an HR initiative.

2) Psychological Safety Shows Up as a Real Retention Lever
Engagement activities (training, 1:1s, peer collaboration) feed into psychological safety and performance in intuitive ways.

In the retention model, psychological safety lands as one of the strongest predictors of whether someone stays, even after controlling for performance.

In practical terms: small, measurable improvements in psychological safety move the odds of retention in a meaningful way, which makes it a good candidate for a monitored KPI.

3) Program Impact Compounds Over Time, Not Just at Launch
In the longitudinal view, program participants improve faster than the control group month over month.

The Group × Month effect tells us the gap widens over time, which is exactly what you’d hope for if you’re investing in capability‑building instead of a one‑off campaign.

Burnout trends move in the opposite direction of productivity, suggesting the program is improving performance without quietly burning people out.

## Why This Matters for Operators and Execs
Support programs as levers: You can treat structured support as a tunable operational lever with measurable upside, not just a perk.

Psychological safety as an early‑warning metric: Safety scores behave like a leading indicator for retention, which means you don’t have to wait for attrition to show up in the lagging metrics.

Look at programs over time, not just at launch: The longitudinal work shows that how outcomes evolve month‑to‑month matters as much as the initial bump.

Connect people decisions to ROI: By putting a dollar value on productivity and layering in cost, this work gives a template for treating workforce initiatives like any other investment.

## How to Use This Work
As a template for evaluating future programs: define the question, design the data, run interpretable models, and end with a simple ROI view.

As a playbook for retention analytics: start with engagement and psychological safety, then connect them to performance and retention outcomes.

As a demo of how I think: frame the business problem first, then choose the method, then translate the results into decisions.
